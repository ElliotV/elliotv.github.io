Water Sources

Lake Brunner and the rivers Whataroa and Perth, located on the west coast of the South Island have been used as water sources

The natural Lake Brunner catchment boasts an impressive area of 470 square kilometers, providing over 1 billion cubic meters of rainfall per annum. This catchment alone has the capability to irrigate over 100,000 hectares of drought land.

The construction of an earth dam where the forks of Whataroa and Perth Rivers meet provides a catchment area of 380 square kilometers for collection. 820 million cubic meters of rainfall per annum falls within this catchment.

Tunnels

A 6.5m Tunnel Boring Machine will be used both initially for Phase One and then for Phase 2

Phase One of the project explores transporting water from Lake Brunner to Lake Coleridge. The lakes lie an elevation of 100m and 500m above sea level, respectively. The water will be pumped through 27km of piping from Lake Brunner up into an elevated reservoir resting at 600m in the Southern Alps. This elevation gain allows for a completely gravity powered water pipe to transport the water from the reservoir through to Lake Coleridge. A 5.0m diameter internal water pipe will lie within tunnel which will be bored through the Southern Alps.

Phase Two of the project involves collecting water in the Whataroa Valley and transporting it to a man-made reservoir in Central Canterbury. This will be done solely through the power of gravity. A 110km tunnel will be bored with the same Tunnel Boring Machine used in Phase One, allowing for a 5.5m diameter internal water pipe to carry the flow.

Distribution

Over 1 billion cubic meters of water will be supplied to the Canterbury Region, irrigation 105,000 hectares of land

Water will be fed from Lake Coleridge into the Rakaia River at a controlled rate, supplementing the irrigation schemes and infrastructure already in place. This will add an additional 25,000 hectares of land to be irrigated by the Rakaia River, with room for expansion, providing for the Northern Canterbury Region.

Water is channeled from the man-made reservoir into the Rangitata River. The Rangitata River provides irrigation to the Central and Southern Canterbury regions, and coupling this with Phase One distribution we will be able to irrigate an additional 105,000 hectares thus alleviating drought issues in Canterbury. A consequent effect of this option is the reduction in the reliance of the Waitaki River to irrigate north into Canterbury, allowing for the primary focus of the Waitaki River irrigation schemes to flow southwards into Otago.

Finance Plan

The project finances will be funded by a number of sources

The project is estimated to cost 6.2 billion dollars. The proportions of investment are as shown above. Design and construction will take place in the first 27 years. Once the project becomes operational, the government will financially assist the private partner by providing 620 million per annum from the 1.2 billion extra tax revenue generated as a result of preventing the drought.

Financial Analysis

Agriculture, Hydropower, local and national system will be analysed

The net present value (NPV) was calculated for 100 years at 2% discount rate. Then a sensitivity test was performed to see how the number of years and discount rates affected the NPV. The balance sheet, profit & loss statement and cash flow statement was generated. Finally the benefit and cost analysis (BCA) was performed. The conclusion drawn is that the project is beneficial, but impossible to execute if financial support was not provided by the government to the private partner.

Economic Analysis

Agriculture, Hydropower, local and national system will be analysed

Economic benefit is analysed in terms of 4 categories: agriculture, hydropower, local and national. Cost of resources, legal issues, construction and labour are also being discussed in detail. It is expected that the regional GDP will increase by 3.01 billion dollars in Canterbury and 1.29 billion dollars in Otago.
